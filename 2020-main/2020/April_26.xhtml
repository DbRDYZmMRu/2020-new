Devotional: THINGS HAPPEN IN TRAINING

 

Poem of the Day: Into my Heart an Air that Kills by A. E. Housman

 

 

 

 

Entries:

 

         Here is a profound insight, two points of view. One from a school of thought, a way of living — Stoicism. And an evolutionary perspective of instinctive response integrated with mythological discernment. The latter from a lecture on the biblical series:  Introduction to the idea of God  by Professor Jordan B Peterson, the former from today’s stoic devotional.

 

         Nature is beautiful. intelligence; beauty with direction. The latter suggest the use of attention and language to confront things that most threaten us. “ If there are things that upset — chaotic, terrible, serpentine, monstrous underworld things that threaten — the best thing is to open your eyes, get your speech organized, and go out and confront the thing, and make the world out of it — cancel out the upsetting object .” My understanding of this view is the instinctive discernment of the archetypal problem using perceptual inference.

 

         The other suggest, I paraphrase: “ When your sparring partner scratches or head-butts you, you don’t then make a show of it, or protest, or view him with suspicion or as plotting against you. And yet you keep an eye on him, not as an enemy or with suspicion, but with a healthy avoidance. You should act this way with all things in life. We should give a pass to many things with our fellow trainees. For, as I’ve said, it’s possible to avoid without suspicion or hate. 

 

          By seeing each day and each situation as a kind of training exercise, the stakes suddenly become a lot lower. The way you interpret your own mistakes and the mistakes of others is suddenly a lot more generous. It’s certainly a more resilient attitude than going around acting like the stakes of every encounter put the championship on the line. 

 

          When you catch an elbow or an unfair blow today, shake off the pain and remind yourself: I’m learning. My sparring partner is learning too. This is practice for both of us—that’s all. I know a bit more about him or her, and from my reaction, they’re going to learn a little bit more about me too. ”

 

         These two perspectives enjoin us on modes of reaction, a dichotomy of perception and action. I seem to be not just a practicing stoic but a budding philosopher.
