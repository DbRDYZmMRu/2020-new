Devotional: THERE’S NOTHING WRONG WITH BEING WRONG

 

Poem of the Day: Robinson Crusoe’s Wise Sayings by Ian McMillan

 

 

 

 

Entries:

 

         Repetitiveness doesn’t breed progress, a personal observation through a self-willed state.

 

         Understanding  civil  implies environmental in certain context is cool.

 

         I love it when I think of the utter unimportance of living a life that isn’t worth living, and a muscle spasm reiterates in consensus. It’s the only reason I need for my second suicide attempt. I should a fix a date, probably before the Coronavirus, give myself a last:  what is on my mind .

 

         I choose not to bother or displease my existence by pondering any of the ephemeral thoughts suggested, carrying out the current task is all that is of importance.

 

         Concentrate on the effect of your actions on your well-being, not others.

 

         The day the story of the lonesome Robinson Crusoe is published by Daniel Defoe in 1719.

 

         Thinking to myself, the constant perturbation of my state of consciousness, the almost perishing of the concept of  me . To think that I come from a lineage of royalty, a supposed dignified, regal race, I doubt it.

 

         Reading the book  Breaking open the head  I come by this idea by R. Gordon Wasson : “ A prodigious expansion in Man’s memory must have been the gift that differentiated mankind from his predecessors, and I surmise that this expansion in memory led to a simultaneous growth in the gift of language, these two powers generating in man that self-consciousness which is the third of the triune traits that alone make man unique. . . . I am asking myself whether Soma could have possessed the power to spark what I have called these triune traits. ” it’s a surmise not a factual theory. Wasson later came to deplore the idea of psychedelia saying “ Although these fungi had produced all of the great philosophic visions of antiquity, he proclaimed they had no relevance to the modern world. ”

 

         I wish my problems were pellucid. I catch myself doing things the wrong way. I want to cry most times, but in the end to live is to know when to die I think.

 

         To think I came home as an act of convalesce, only to deteriorate to a state of almost madness is beyond sad I . . .
